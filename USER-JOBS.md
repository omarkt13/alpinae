# User jobs — the source of truth for wording and visuals

Every sentence of copy and every visual on the site and in the demo must serve
one of these jobs. If a phrase explains *our strategy* rather than doing *their
job*, it's internal leakage — cut it.

---

## The advisor (relationship manager / partner)

**Who:** manages 20–60 private-client relationships at a Geneva or Luxembourg
firm. Quarter-end is a reporting crunch. Excel-native, time-poor,
reputation-driven. Deepest professional fear: a wrong number, or another
client's data, reaching a client or a regulator with their name on it.

### Functional jobs

| # | When… | I want to… | So that… |
|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | quarter-end statements arrive | get each file into the right client's space and be certain nothing crossed between clients | I never have to explain a mix-up |
| A2 | a client asks "where do I stand?" | see their total consolidated position immediately | I sound in command of their affairs |
| A3 | I assemble the quarterly report | have the consolidated view land in our own format, untouched by hand | the cycle takes hours, not days |
| A4 | a figure looks off | see exactly which statement, page, and line it came from, and the FX applied | I can defend or correct it on the spot |
| A5 | a bank reissues a corrected statement | swap it in without losing the original or its history | the file holds up under audit |
| A6 | compliance or an auditor asks | show who ingested what, who approved what, who saw what | the answer is an export, not an archaeology project |

### Emotional job
Be — and be seen as — meticulous. The tool must *transfer* diligence to them,
never appear to replace it. Hence: the human always approves; the tool always
shows its work.

### Consequences for wording
- Their vocabulary: *client, mandate, custodian, statement, valuation date,
  position, exposure, report, approve*.
- Banned on anything advisor-facing: *workflow, pipeline, AI, sync, platform,
  autonomous, codebase, by construction* — engineer/strategy diction.
- Sentences state what happens to *their clients and their reports*, not how
  the system is built.

### Consequences for visuals
- Numbers carry the page: large, tabular, monospaced. Prose supports.
- Status at a glance: what needs *my decision* is visually first.
- Every figure has a visible provenance affordance (A4).
- No decorative dashboards. A chart earns its place only if it answers
  A2 faster than the number alone.

## The end client (and the next generation)

**Who:** the private client — often senior, wealth handled by the firm for
years; increasingly their adult children, who expect to check things online.
Not finance professionals. They read for **two minutes**, on trust.
The portal is switched on *by the firm*, for clients who want it.

### Functional jobs

| # | When… | I want to… | So that… |
|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | I open my page | see what I'm worth, in one number | I know where I stand |
| C2 | I glance further | see whether it went up or down — since last quarter and over the year | I sense direction without reading tables |
| C3 | I look once more | see where my money is — what kinds of assets, which banks, which currencies | I feel informed, not managed in the dark |
| C4 | I wonder if it's right | see that my manager approved these figures, and when | I trust the page like I trust the letter |
| C5 | (next-gen) it's 22:00 on a Sunday | check without calling anyone | I stay informed on my terms |

### Anti-jobs (deliberately unserved)
No trading, no what-if tools, no risk metrics jargon (volatility, VaR, Sharpe),
no notifications, no interactivity beyond looking. It is a beautifully typeset
statement that happens to be a web page.

### Consequences for wording
- Plain, warm, formal: *"Your consolidated position", "prepared by",
  "approved by your manager on…"*.
- Every value visibly dated (C4). No jargon, no product talk, no "Alpinae"
  prominence — the *firm's* name leads; we are the paper it's printed on.

### Consequences for visuals
- One large number (C1), with its change stated right beneath (C2).
- **Quick-glance charts, exactly three:** value over 12 months (C2),
  allocation by asset class (C3), currency exposure (C3). By-bank stays a
  small table — names matter more than proportions there.
- Charts are calm: single accent colour, hairline grid, no legends where a
  direct label works, nothing animated.

## Litmus tests

1. **Advisor page:** would Olivier show this screen to a client mid-meeting
   without embarrassment? Would he trust it in front of an auditor?
2. **Client page:** could a 74-year-old read everything in two minutes, and
   would her daughter still find it modern?
3. **Any sentence:** does it do a job from the tables above? If it explains
   our positioning, market analysis, or engineering, it goes.
